How to Read a Nautical Chart: A Beginner's Study Plan

A chart becomes less intimidating when you read it in layers: source and scale first, water and hazards second, aids and routes third, then the notes that control interpretation.

Reviewed

July 21, 2026

Use with

Current official charts, U.S. Chart No. 1, Coast Pilot, Notices to Mariners, and qualified instruction

Start with what a chart can—and cannot—tell you

NOAA describes nautical charts as specialized maps showing water depths, shoreline, prominent features, aids to navigation, and other information useful to marine navigation. A chart is not a promise that conditions are unchanged, nor is it a substitute for a complete voyage plan, current corrections, sensors, lookout, or professional judgment.

Use training charts only for training. For real navigation, follow command requirements and verify that every required product, correction, and system is current and authorized.

Never navigate from this article

This guide teaches a study method, not a safe track. Do not use screenshots, website graphics, or remembered symbols for actual navigation.

Read the chart's identity before its symbols

Before interpreting a feature, learn the chart's framework. Locate and explain:

  • Chart or cell identification and geographic coverage
  • Edition, update information, and source notes
  • Scale and the amount of detail appropriate to that scale
  • Horizontal datum used for positions
  • Sounding and height units
  • Vertical datum used for depths and heights
  • Projection and relevant cautionary notes

Practice saying why each item matters. A correct sounding interpreted in the wrong unit or against the wrong datum is not a correct understanding.

Study water in layers

Begin with the broad depth picture, then zoom into individual values. Identify depth areas, contour lines, spot soundings, drying heights, and the reference datum. Ask what is directly charted and what would require calculation, current environmental information, or command-approved tools.

Do not treat blue or white coloring as a universal “safe” or “unsafe” answer. Color presentation varies with the product and display settings; vessel limits and current conditions determine operational meaning.

Separate fixed hazards from aids to navigation

Build two lists while studying. The first contains hazards and physical features—rocks, wrecks, obstructions, shoals, cables, pipelines, and restricted areas. The second contains aids intended to help a mariner—lights, buoys, beacons, daymarks, ranges, and other marks.

For every symbol, use U.S. Chart No. 1 to identify the symbol, abbreviation, color, characteristic, and qualifying note. Avoid inventing meaning from the picture alone.

Read labels as carefully as symbols

Many chart features combine a symbol with letters, numbers, colors, light characteristics, or explanatory notes. Train yourself to read the complete feature from left to right and then verify it in the reference.

  • What object or feature is shown?
  • What attributes are encoded in the label?
  • Which part is a measurement, identifier, characteristic, or warning?
  • Does an adjoining note modify the interpretation?
  • Is the display showing paper-chart or electronic-chart symbology?

NOAA's U.S. Chart No. 1 presents symbols, abbreviations, and terms for both paper and electronic navigational chart data. Learn to recognize that the same underlying feature can be portrayed differently by different systems.

Use a 30-minute study loop

  • Five minutes: inspect identity, scale, units, datums, and notes.
  • Ten minutes: select ten unfamiliar features and look each up in U.S. Chart No. 1.
  • Five minutes: describe the broad depth and hazard picture without planning a route.
  • Five minutes: compare paper and ENC presentations for several features.
  • Five minutes: write an error log: symbol missed, assumption made, source checked, corrected interpretation.

Repeat with a different area and scale. Mixed practice prevents you from memorizing one harbor instead of learning chart language.

Ask four questions during every chart exercise

  1. What is the source and currency of this information?
  2. What unit, datum, or scale controls the value?
  3. Which official reference confirms my interpretation?
  4. What information is missing from the chart alone?

The fourth question matters most. Weather, tides, currents, Notices to Mariners, local restrictions, equipment status, vessel characteristics, and command direction may all be necessary to understand the operating picture.
