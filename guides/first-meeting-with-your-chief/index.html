Your First Meeting With Your Chief

Your first conversation does not need to solve every problem. It should establish how you will learn, communicate, make decisions, and follow through together.

Reviewed

July 21, 2026

Use with

Your chain of command, command instructions, division turnover, and your chief's experience

Start with the right objective

Your objective is to begin a professional working relationship—not to prove that you already know how the division should run. You bring commissioned authority and responsibility; your chief brings leadership experience, technical perspective, and knowledge of the Sailors and command. The division needs both of you aligned.

The Navy Leader Development Framework emphasizes competence, character, and connections. A useful first meeting supports all three: learn the work, make your standards clear, and build the trust required for candid communication.

Listen before changing

Unless safety, legality, or an immediate order requires action, learn why the current system exists before replacing it. Ask what has already been tried and what constraints you cannot yet see.

Prepare a one-page agenda

Ask for enough uninterrupted time to talk privately. Bring a notebook suitable for unclassified administrative notes, but keep the meeting conversational. A simple agenda is enough:

  • Your background, current knowledge gaps, and what you hope to learn first.
  • The division's mission, people, current priorities, and recurring friction.
  • How the two of you will communicate, disagree, and make decisions.
  • The commitments and deadlines already in motion.
  • A short list of actions to complete before your follow-up meeting.

Review any authorized turnover material beforehand. Do not copy operational, personnel, maintenance, or controlled information into personal devices, public tools, or unofficial cloud services.

Ask questions that reveal the division

Open questions help your chief show you the real operating picture. Useful prompts include:

  • What is this division most proud of?
  • What are the top three priorities right now?
  • What recurring problem consumes the most time?
  • What do our Sailors need from me during my first week?
  • Which commitments, inspections, qualifications, or deadlines are already underway?
  • Who are the key people I should meet, and what should I learn from each?
  • What is one thing a new division officer often misunderstands here?

Do not ask for sensitive personal details about Sailors unless you have an official need to know. Let your chief identify issues that should move to the appropriate command, medical, legal, security, or support channel.

Agree on how you will work together

Ambiguity creates avoidable friction. Discuss a practical rhythm for routine updates, urgent issues, task tracking, and feedback. Ask how your chief prefers to raise concerns and explain how you want to be informed when your assumptions are wrong.

  • Routine: decide when you will conduct a short daily sync and a longer weekly review.
  • Urgent matters: define what requires immediate notification, consistent with command direction.
  • Decisions: identify which choices you can delegate, which require consultation, and which must go up the chain.
  • Disagreement: commit to candid, respectful discussion—preferably in private—without hiding material concerns.
  • Follow-through: keep one shared or command-approved system for owners, deadlines, and status.
Authority does not remove accountability

You remain responsible for your decisions. Seek your chief's counsel, understand the applicable instruction, and elevate issues through the chain when required.

Avoid five early mistakes

  • Performing expertise: pretending to understand discourages correction and slows your learning.
  • Announcing immediate reforms: a first impression is not a diagnosis.
  • Treating your chief as an administrative assistant: use the relationship for leadership, judgment, and development—not just task routing.
  • Bypassing the relationship: repeatedly directing Sailors without keeping your chief informed can create confusion.
  • Avoiding hard conversations: delayed feedback usually becomes a larger problem. Be specific, private, and professional.

Close with three commitments

End by repeating what you heard. Record no more than three immediate commitments, each with an owner and due date. Then schedule a follow-up within roughly a week to compare your first impressions with what you have learned.

A strong closing can be simple: “Here is what I heard, here is what I will do, and here is when we will check back.” Reliability will build more credibility than a polished speech.
