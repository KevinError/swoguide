Essential Books and Public References for New SWOs

Start with current official sources and the material your command issues. Buy a book only when it fills a specific gap.

Reviewed

July 22, 2026

Best first move

Ask your course or command which edition and format it expects before purchasing anything.

Do not begin with a shopping spree

There is no universal stack of books that every new Surface Warfare Officer must personally own. Your orders, schoolhouse guidance, command instructions, issued publications, and qualification materials control what you actually need. Some references change, some are available free from official sources, and some will already be provided.

Use this list to organize your study—not to replace a command reading list or create an unofficial requirement.

Protect controlled information

Do not upload, photograph, or share ship-specific instructions, PQS material, watchstation products, diagrams, schedules, or operational details. Use only properly authorized systems and follow your command's handling rules.

Tier 1: Navigation Rules and Regulations Handbook

The U.S. Coast Guard Navigation Center publishes the current handbook covering the International and U.S. Inland Navigation Rules and related regulations. It should be your primary public source for Rules of the Road study. Use the official text first; flash cards, quizzes, and commercial study aids are secondary tools.

  • Keep a searchable official copy for reference.
  • Confirm whether a question applies to International Rules, Inland Rules, or both.
  • Track amendments and use the current edition linked by the Coast Guard.
  • Pair reading with scenarios and an error log rather than memorizing answer letters.

Open the Coast Guard's amalgamated Navigation Rules or use its Navigation Rules and Regulations Handbook.

Tier 1: U.S. Chart No. 1

NOAA describes U.S. Chart No. 1 as the reference for symbols, abbreviations, and terms used on nautical charts. It includes portrayals used for paper products and electronic navigational chart data. Use it to verify a feature instead of guessing from its appearance.

  • Practice looking up symbols by category.
  • Read the full abbreviation or characteristic attached to a symbol.
  • Compare paper and electronic portrayals where they differ.
  • Use current chart products and corrections for real navigation.

Open NOAA's U.S. Chart No. 1 page.

Tier 1: The American Practical Navigator

The American Practical Navigator, commonly called Bowditch, is a broad navigation reference published through the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency. It is most useful as an explanation source: consult the relevant chapter when you need the principles behind a method, instrument, calculation, chart concept, or navigation practice.

Do not try to read it cover to cover before reporting. Bookmark the official portal, learn its structure, and use it when a course topic or qualification item gives you a focused question.

Open NGA's American Practical Navigator portal.

Tier 1: U.S. Coast Pilot

NOAA's United States Coast Pilot supplements nautical charts with narrative information for coastal and intracoastal waters. It includes subjects such as channels, anchorages, hazards, weather, regulations, and port facilities. Treat it as a current voyage-planning reference, not a book to memorize.

Study how to locate information and verify the applicable volume and updates. For operations, use only the current, command-authorized products and planning process.

Open NOAA's United States Coast Pilot page.

Tier 2: Material your command issues

Your highest-value working references will often be provided after you arrive. Examples may include command instructions, approved watchstation references, qualification materials, standing orders, and locally required study products. Their availability, content, and handling requirements vary.

  1. Ask what you are authorized and expected to use.
  2. Confirm the current version and where the controlled copy lives.
  3. Build a private index of topics and official locations—not a personal archive of controlled files.
  4. Write questions for qualified instructors and your chain of command.

Tier 3: Optional commercial books

A commercial study guide can be useful when it adds clearer diagrams, practice problems, or a format that helps you review. It is never more authoritative than the governing source. Before buying one, check:

  • Currency: Does it match the current rules and expected edition?
  • Purpose: Does it solve a specific study problem?
  • Source trail: Can you verify its answers against official material?
  • Availability: Can you borrow it or preview it first?
  • Command fit: Does your schoolhouse or command recommend a different resource?

Avoid collecting books merely because someone else owned them. Outdated certainty is worse than a current reference you know how to use.

A lean 30-day reference plan

  • Week 1: Save the official Navigation Rules, Chart No. 1, Bowditch, and Coast Pilot links. Learn what each source answers.
  • Week 2: Use the Rules handbook for short daily scenario practice and record missed concepts.
  • Week 3: Look up chart symbols and read one focused Bowditch section tied to current coursework.
  • Week 4: Ask your command or instructor which issued materials and optional books belong in your next study block.

The goal is not a full shelf. It is a small set of current sources that you can navigate confidently and verify against official direction.

Official starting points

  • U.S. Coast Guard: Amalgamated Navigation Rules
  • NOAA: U.S. Chart No. 1
  • NGA: The American Practical Navigator
  • NOAA: United States Coast Pilot
