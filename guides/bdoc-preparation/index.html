What BDOC Covers and How to Prepare

BDOC is your first structured introduction to the knowledge, habits, and judgment expected of a Surface Warfare Officer. The goal is not to arrive knowing everything—it is to arrive ready to learn well.

Reviewed

July 21, 2026

Use with

Your orders, current CANTRAC information, schoolhouse guidance, and official course materials

What BDOC is

The Basic Division Officer Course is an early training milestone in the Surface Warfare Officer pipeline. Surface Warfare Schools Command currently describes instruction at its San Diego and Norfolk BDOC locations as covering leadership, management, navigation, seamanship, shiphandling, engineering, and maritime warfighting.

Public Navy coverage from 2022 described BDOC as a nine-week course. Course length, sequencing, reporting procedures, and local requirements can change, so treat your orders, current CANTRAC information, and schoolhouse messages as authoritative—not an old article or an unofficial study guide.

Verify the current version

Before making travel, lodging, uniform, or study-material decisions, confirm details with your sponsor, orders, and the appropriate Mariner Skills Training Center.

What you can expect to study

The public course descriptions point to a deliberately broad foundation. You are preparing to lead Sailors, understand a ship as a system, and develop safe mariner skills—not specialize in only one subject.

  • Division leadership and administration: the fundamentals of working with a chief, communicating expectations, tracking division responsibilities, and leading in an operational environment.
  • Navigation and maritime law: foundational navigation concepts, Rules of the Road, bridge-team communication, and the judgment needed to operate safely.
  • Seamanship and shiphandling: terminology, basic principles, and supervised practice that prepare you for later watchstanding instruction.
  • Engineering and ship systems: a foundational view of how major systems support readiness and operations.
  • Damage control: responsibilities, organization, communication, and the disciplined use of approved procedures.
  • Maritime warfighting and weapons fundamentals: an introduction to how surface forces operate and how warfare areas connect.

Navy reporting also describes simulator-based learning, including the Conning Officer Virtual Environment, as part of building navigation and shiphandling experience. Treat simulator time as practice in communication and decision-making, not as a video game or a contest to finish first.

Prepare before the first day

The best preparation is administrative readiness plus strong learning habits. Avoid spending heavily on unofficial courses, answer banks, or large collections of notes whose currency and authorization you cannot verify.

  • Read your orders and every message from the schoolhouse carefully.
  • Confirm reporting location, time, uniform, lodging, transportation, and required documents.
  • Set up one notebook or authorized digital system with sections for concepts, questions, mistakes, and follow-ups.
  • Refresh basic nautical vocabulary and navigation concepts using authorized public or issued material.
  • Build a consistent sleep and exercise routine before the course begins.
  • Bring required uniform items in serviceable condition and leave room to adjust after receiving current guidance.
Do not chase leaked material

Use only authorized course resources. Do not seek, store, or share controlled exams, answer keys, internal courseware, ship-specific information, or material you are not authorized to possess.

Use a daily learning cycle

BDOC covers several unfamiliar subjects quickly. A repeatable study loop is more useful than occasional marathon sessions.

  • Preview: spend a few minutes identifying the lesson’s vocabulary and main question.
  • Listen actively: record relationships and decision points instead of trying to transcribe every slide.
  • Teach it back: explain the concept aloud without looking at your notes, then identify what you missed.
  • Practice: apply the idea through approved problems, diagrams, or simulator scenarios.
  • Debrief: write the specific error, why it happened, and what you will do differently next time.
  • Revisit: review the topic after one day and again after one week.

When you are confused, ask a narrow question. “Can you walk me through the cue that should have changed my decision?” produces better learning than “I don’t understand any of this.”

Make simulator time count

Before a scenario, identify the learning objective and your communication responsibilities. During the scenario, prioritize a shared mental picture, clear standard phraseology, and safe decisions. Afterward, separate technical mistakes from communication mistakes.

Keep an error log with three columns: what happened, what cue you missed, and the corrected action or principle. Patterns will emerge. You may discover that the real issue is not knowledge, but task prioritization, late communication, or failing to verify an assumption.

Leave with a bridge to your first ship

BDOC cannot teach the configuration, procedures, and culture of every ship. Keep a final list of topics you understand in principle but must relearn using your ship’s current instructions and qualified personnel.

Before graduation, organize your notes into a short transition list: concepts to retain, questions for your sponsor, habits that worked, and weaknesses that need deliberate practice. Do not carry controlled course content into unauthorized storage or public tools.
